The Inflation is Just Right

There are a lot of factors that determine the price of a loaf of bread, orange juice, or chocolate bar. One of those factors is inflation, or the amount of goods you can buy with a predetermined amount of currency. When prices rise, it is called inflation. When the opposite happens, it's called deflation. For most economies some level of inflation is good- the Bank of England for example has a yearly goal of 2 per cent inflation per year. But how exactly does this rather confusing economic term work and how does it make your grocery price go up?

To understand that, we first need to understand what money is and how it works. Money is a value of exchange or something we use to determine what something is worth. Inflation happens when the money or value is not worth as much, hence the reason why prices increase.

One factor that governments use to determine inflation is tracking prices of a set number of goods that the average household buys, called the Basket of Goods. But this is where it gets a little tricky. Just because a price of a good goes up does not always mean it is because of inflation. Other factors, such as weather, could cause prices of a specific item to go up. However, when the average price of many goods go up, coupled with other factors in the economy such as borrowing costs, it is usually a sign of inflation.
